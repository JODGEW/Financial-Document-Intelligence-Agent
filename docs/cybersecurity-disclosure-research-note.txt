ACME CORPORATION — INTERNAL RESEARCH NOTE
Cybersecurity Risk Disclosure Trends in Financial Technology Filings
Prepared by: Research & Analytics Team
Date: November 18, 2025
Classification: Internal Use Only

------------------------------------------------------------------------

SUMMARY

This note reviews cybersecurity risk disclosure patterns across 42 financial
technology companies that filed 10-K annual reports with the SEC between
January and October 2025. The review was prompted by the SEC's December 2023
cybersecurity disclosure rules (Release No. 33-11216), which require public
companies to disclose material cybersecurity incidents within four business
days of determining materiality, and to provide annual disclosures about
cybersecurity risk management, strategy, and governance.

The findings are intended to inform Acme's own disclosure drafting and
governance practices ahead of the fiscal year 2025 annual report filing.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

SECTION 1: REGULATORY BACKGROUND

The SEC adopted its cybersecurity disclosure rules in July 2023, effective for
annual reports filed after December 15, 2023. The rules impose two primary
disclosure obligations on public companies:

1. INCIDENT DISCLOSURE (Form 8-K, Item 1.05)
   Companies must disclose material cybersecurity incidents within four
   business days of determining that the incident is material. The disclosure
   must describe the material aspects of the incident's nature, scope, and
   timing, and its material impact or reasonably likely material impact.

2. ANNUAL RISK MANAGEMENT DISCLOSURE (Form 10-K, Item 1C)
   Companies must describe their processes for assessing, identifying, and
   managing material risks from cybersecurity threats, the material effects of
   cybersecurity risks and incidents on the company's business strategy and
   financial condition, and the board's oversight of cybersecurity risks.

The rules do not prescribe specific security controls or require disclosure of
technical vulnerability details that could themselves create security risks.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

SECTION 2: DISCLOSURE PATTERNS OBSERVED

2.1 Board Oversight

Of the 42 companies reviewed, 38 (90%) disclosed that the Audit Committee or
a dedicated Risk Committee of the Board has primary oversight responsibility
for cybersecurity risks. Four companies disclosed that full board oversight
was retained rather than delegated to a committee.

The most common disclosure pattern describes:
  - Frequency of CISO or senior management briefings to the board committee
    (typically quarterly, with ad hoc briefings for material incidents)
  - Board member qualifications or experience relevant to cybersecurity
  - Process by which management escalates cybersecurity issues to the board

2.2 Incident History Disclosures

Twenty-one companies (50%) disclosed at least one cybersecurity incident or
attempted intrusion in their fiscal year 2025 filings. Of these:

  - 17 disclosed incidents that were determined not to be material
  - 3 disclosed incidents that were determined material and filed Form 8-K
  - 1 disclosed an incident under investigation with materiality pending

No company in the review sample disclosed a material incident that involved
confirmed compromise of client financial data. Three companies disclosed
incidents involving unauthorized access to employee credentials or internal
systems with no client data affected.

2.3 Insurance Coverage Disclosures

Thirty-one companies (74%) disclosed the existence of cyber liability insurance.
Of those, 18 disclosed the aggregate coverage amount, which ranged from
$10 million to $200 million per incident. The median disclosed coverage amount
was $50 million. Coverage amounts above $100 million were limited to companies
with annual revenues exceeding $500 million.

2.4 Third-Party and Supply Chain Risk

The most significant growth in disclosure depth year over year was in
third-party and supply chain risk language. Forty companies (95%) included
explicit disclosure of risks from third-party vendors or service providers,
compared with 71% in the prior year review. The shift reflects increased
regulatory and investor focus following widely reported supply chain
vulnerabilities in 2024.

Common language describes:
  - Vendor security assessment and onboarding procedures
  - Contractual security requirements imposed on vendors
  - Ongoing monitoring and right-to-audit provisions
  - Concentration risk from reliance on major cloud infrastructure providers

------------------------------------------------------------------------

SECTION 3: GOVERNANCE OBSERVATIONS

3.1 CISO Reporting Structure

Among companies disclosing the CISO's reporting line, 24 reported to the
Chief Technology Officer, 11 reported directly to the CEO, and 7 reported
to the Chief Risk Officer or General Counsel. Direct-to-CEO reporting was
more common at companies that had experienced a material incident in the
prior three years.

3.2 Tabletop Exercises and Testing

Thirty companies disclosed the use of tabletop exercises, penetration testing,
or red team engagements. Disclosure of third-party penetration testing was
more common among companies with market capitalizations above $1 billion.

SECTION 4: IMPLICATIONS FOR ACME'S FISCAL YEAR 2025 DISCLOSURE

Based on the patterns reviewed, the following are areas where Acme's
Item 1C disclosure may benefit from additional specificity:

1. BOARD BRIEFING FREQUENCY
   Disclosing the specific cadence of CISO briefings to the Audit and Risk
   Committee (quarterly, as currently practiced) is now a market standard.
   Consider adding explicit language to this effect in Item 1C.

2. INCIDENT REMEDIATION LANGUAGE
   For the two attempted intrusions detected in FY2025, disclosure should
   clearly state the outcome (no data compromised), describe containment
   measures, and confirm that neither was determined to be material under
   the SEC's four-day reporting standard.

3. INSURANCE COVERAGE
   Acme discloses $50 million in aggregate cyber liability coverage. This is
   at the median for the peer group by coverage amount. Consider whether
   disclosure of the coverage amount serves Acme's disclosure objectives
   or is better omitted, given that it also signals coverage limits to
   potential adversaries.

4. THIRD-PARTY RISK
   Acme's current Item 1C language is brief on vendor risk management. Given
   the 95% disclosure rate among peers and increased investor focus on supply
   chain risk, expanding this section is recommended before the final filing.

5. ZERO-TRUST ARCHITECTURE
   Acme implemented zero-trust network architecture in Q3 2025. This is a
   specific, verifiable control. Disclosing named architectural approaches
   is a disclosure choice, not a requirement. Discuss with outside counsel
   whether including it is appropriate given the detail it provides about
   internal controls.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

SECTION 5: SOURCES AND METHODOLOGY

This review analyzed Item 1C disclosures from 42 public financial technology
companies with annual revenues between $50 million and $2 billion that filed
Form 10-K with the SEC between January 1, 2025 and October 31, 2025. Filings
were retrieved from the SEC EDGAR database. Companies were selected to
represent a peer group comparable to Acme by revenue, business model, and
regulatory profile.

This note does not constitute legal advice. Disclosure decisions should be
made in consultation with outside securities counsel.
